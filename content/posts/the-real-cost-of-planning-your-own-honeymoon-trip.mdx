## The time tax nobody talks about

Here's what 15 years of watching couples plan honeymoons has taught me: the average person spends 23 hours researching and booking a week-long honeymoon. That's nearly three full workdays browsing hotel comparison sites, reading contradictory TripAdvisor reviews, and second-guessing American Airlines versus Delta departure times.

If you're earning $75,000 annually, those 23 hours represent $1,106 of your time. Suddenly, that "free" planning doesn't look so appealing.

> "The most expensive honeymoons aren't the ones with the highest price tags—they're the ones that steal weeks of your life during an already overwhelming time."

But the time calculation goes deeper than pure hours. Wedding planning already demands 100+ hours of your attention. Adding honeymoon research to that load creates decision fatigue that affects both choices poorly.

I watched a couple spend three weeks agonizing between the Katikies Hotel and Canaves Oia Hotel in Santorini, only to book the wrong dates at Katikies and pay $400 in change fees. The "savings" from doing it themselves vanished instantly.

## The amateur booking premium

Professional travel bookers access Virtuoso rates and Four Seasons Preferred Partner inventory you'll never see on Expedia. But even using the same tools, experience matters enormously.

Last month, I compared bookings made by first-time honeymoon planners versus what Otherwhere achieved for similar trips to Maui, the Maldives, and Tuscany. The DIY bookings cost 18% more on average, even when travelers thought they'd found "deals."

Common expensive mistakes include:

• Booking Delta flights and Grand Wailea separately instead of packages ($400+ premium)
• Missing Basic Economy restrictions that prevent upgrades
• Choosing Conrad Maldives during Chinese New Year (rates double to $2,800/night)
• Booking non-refundable rates at Belmond without understanding 72-hour cancellation policies
• Ignoring monsoon season in Thailand that drops rates by 40%

The most costly error? Booking too early or too late. United flights to Tahiti follow predictable patterns, but most people book emotionally rather than strategically.

> "Amateur bookers consistently pay premium rates because they don't understand the invisible architecture of travel pricing—like how Air France releases award space exactly 355 days out, while Delta waits until 330 days."

One couple I know booked their Bora Bora flights on Air Tahiti Nui eight months early, thinking they'd secured a deal at $3,200 each. The same flights cost $1,950 per person six weeks before departure—exactly when professional bookers typically lock in rates for that route.

## The time tax of travel planning

Here's what actually happens when you decide to book that spontaneous weekend escape to Austin. You open one tab for Southwest flights—just to get a sense of pricing, you tell yourself. Two hours later, you're deep in the Flyertalk forums debating whether American's 7:15am departure from LaGuardia is worth the extra $47 over Delta's 2:30pm option that gets you into Austin-Bergstrom at dinnertime.

The average person spends 3.5 hours planning a domestic weekend trip, according to recent travel industry data. But that's spread across multiple sessions over several days. The mental load is what kills you.

You start looking Tuesday afternoon. Check Southwest and American prices Wednesday during lunch. Get distracted by work until Friday, only to discover your perfectly priced $180 nonstop is gone. Weekend rates kick in—now you're looking at $280 for the same route. You panic-book something suboptimal because you've already committed to the trip in your head.

> "The most expensive travel booking isn't the first-class upgrade—it's the opportunity cost of your own research time multiplied by decision paralysis."

I watched a colleague spend four hours last month trying to book San Francisco to Portland for a Willamette Valley wine weekend. She's a marketing director billing $150/hour to clients. Her "savings" from not using a booking service? Maybe $50 on a slightly cheaper hotel. Her actual cost? $600 in billable time, plus the stress headache from comparing 47 different properties in the Pearl District alone.

## The compound cost of choice overload

Portland has 847 hotels listed on Booking.com. Even filtering by the Pearl District and 4+ stars, you're looking at 23 properties. Each requires investigation: walking distance to Powell's Books via Google Street View, parsing TripAdvisor reviews from last month versus 2019, cross-referencing Instagram photos that may or may not reflect recent renovations.

Choice overload is real. Barry Schwartz documented this in "The Paradox of Choice"—too many options lead to decision paralysis and regret. In travel, this translates to hours scrolling through properties that all blur together after the fifth boutique hotel with exposed brick and local art.

The worst part? After all that research, you'll probably pick one of the first three options you saw anyway. That's the psychology of satisficing—we tell ourselves we want the optimal choice, but we settle for "good enough" once we've exhausted our decision-making energy.

Meanwhile, someone who knows Portland's hotel landscape could have narrowed your options to three properties in five minutes. The Kimpton RiverPlace if you want Willamette River views and easy access to the Saturday Market. AC Hotel Portland Downtown for modern efficiency near Pioneer Square. McMenamins Kennedy School if you want to sleep in a converted elementary school with on-site brewery.

## The stress premium nobody talks about

There's an emotional cost to travel planning that's harder to quantify but impossible to ignore. The low-level anxiety of wondering if the Lotte New York Palace is actually worth $150 more per night than the Benjamin. The fear that you've missed some obvious better option that locals know about. The nagging worry that your Midtown hotel will be nothing like the carefully curated Instagram photos.

I used to screenshot hotel lobbies obsessively, creating comparison galleries on my phone. Cross-referencing rates across Booking.com, Hotels.com, and direct booking sites. Setting Google Flights price alerts that would ping me during client presentations with updates on JFK to Charleston routes.

The mental bandwidth consumed by travel planning doesn't end when you click "book." It continues until you're actually standing in your hotel lobby, relieved that The Spectator's parlor actually matches the photos and you're not stuck in some dated property near the cruise terminal.

Professional curation eliminates this anxiety.

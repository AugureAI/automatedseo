## The hidden cost of DIY business travel

Most professionals dramatically underestimate how much time they spend planning business trips. A recent survey by the Global Business Travel Association found that executives spend an average of 4.2 hours researching and booking each business trip—and that's just for domestic travel.

For international trips, that number jumps to 7.5 hours. When you're juggling visa requirements, time zones, and airline alliances, those browser tabs multiply faster than you can manage them.

> "I realized I was spending my Sunday mornings comparing flight times instead of being present with my family. The cost-benefit analysis was embarrassing when I saw I'd burned $1,200 worth of time to save $80 on airfare."

Take Sarah, a partner at McKinsey who travels twice monthly between Chicago and client sites. At $300 per billable hour, her 4.2 hours of trip planning represents $1,260 in opportunity cost per trip. Over a year, she's burning $30,240 worth of time on tasks that could be delegated for under $3,000.

The kicker? She was often making suboptimal choices anyway, booking flights that looked good at 11 PM but created unnecessary layovers at Dallas-Fort Worth or missed her United Premier Gold status benefits entirely.

## When corporations can't keep up

Corporate travel departments sound like the obvious solution, but they're increasingly overwhelmed. The average corporate travel manager now handles bookings for 847 employees, up 23% from pre-pandemic levels according to Business Travel News.

Even when companies provide booking tools like Concur or Egencia, they're often clunky enterprise platforms that prioritize cost control over user experience. These systems frequently miss obvious optimizations—like booking the 7 AM American flight from LaGuardia that gets you home for dinner instead of the red-eye through Phoenix that saves $47.

Many executives find themselves working around their company's travel policies anyway. They'll book the mandated flights through corporate channels, then upgrade themselves or switch from the policy-approved Hampton Inn near Newark Airport to the Conrad Midtown Manhattan where they can actually walk to client meetings.

> "Our corporate Concur system books the cheapest option by default. But 'cheapest' doesn't account for the client dinner at Le Bernardin I'll miss if I take that 4 PM departure instead of the 6:30 PM flight."

This creates a weird dual system where professionals get the worst of both worlds—bureaucratic corporate booking plus personal research time to find alternatives.

## What to look for in travel concierge services

Not all concierge services are created equal. The best ones combine human expertise with modern technology, giving you both personalized service and access to real-time inventory through Amadeus or Sabre GDS systems.

**Key features that matter:**

- **Actual booking capability:** Many services stop at recommendations, leaving you to handle the purchase yourself through airline websites
- **Loyalty program integration:** Your United 1K status and Chase Sapphire benefits should transfer properly with correct frequent flyer numbers
- **Transparent pricing:** Fees built into competitive rates, not surprise $75 charges appearing after booking
- **Speed:** Ability to hold American flights for 24 hours while you decide, not just send static screenshots

The most sophisticated services use APIs that connect directly to airline inventory systems, ensuring you see the same prices and availability as booking direct through united.com or delta.com. This eliminates the frustrating experience of receiving recommendations for $289 flights that jumped to $456 by the time you click through.

Response time matters too. Business travel often involves last-minute changes, and you need someone who can rebook efficiently when your Thursday client presentation gets moved to Tuesday afternoon.

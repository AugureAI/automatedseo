If you're earning six figures, spending three hours researching flights to save $200 is effectively paying yourself $67 an hour to be a travel agent. For most professionals, that's a steep pay cut. The real cost of DIY business travel planning isn't just the time you spend—it's the opportunity cost of what else you could accomplish in those hours.

Let's audit exactly where your time goes and calculate the true economics of business trip planning.

---

## The time drain of business travel planning

Most people dramatically underestimate how long trip planning actually takes. A recent study by the Global Business Travel Association found that the average business traveler spends 4.2 hours planning each trip across multiple sessions.

Here's where those hours disappear:

• **Flight research**: 90 minutes across Kayak, Expedia, airline websites, Google Flights, and Momondo
• **Hotel comparison**: 75 minutes reading TripAdvisor reviews and checking Booking.com amenities  
• **Schedule coordination**: 45 minutes ensuring flights align with client meetings and conference schedules
• **Loyalty program optimization**: 30 minutes checking United Premier status benefits and Hilton Honors upgrades
• **Rebooking and changes**: 40 minutes average when plans shift due to client rescheduling

That's before you factor in the mental switching costs. Every time you toggle from a client presentation to Kayak, you're losing momentum on higher-value work.

> "Time is the scarcest resource and unless it is managed nothing else can be managed." — Peter Drucker

## Calculate your true hourly rate

The math is straightforward, but most professionals don't run the numbers. Take your total annual compensation—salary plus bonus plus benefits—and divide by your actual working hours.

If you're making $150,000 total compensation and work 50 hours per week for 48 weeks, your true hourly rate is $62.50. Every hour you spend on Expedia is an hour you're not billing clients, developing business, or advancing strategic projects.

For senior executives, the numbers become even starker. A VP earning $300,000 has an hourly rate of $125. Spending five hours planning a conference trip to Las Vegas costs $625 in opportunity cost—often more than any savings from finding the "best deal."

> "The wealthy buy time. The poor sell it."

## The compound cost of travel planning mistakes

DIY planning doesn't just cost time upfront—it creates expensive downstream problems. Business travelers who book their own trips are 40% more likely to need changes, according to data from corporate travel managers.

Common expensive mistakes include:

**Booking non-refundable rates to save $50**, then paying $400 change fees when the client meeting moves. A McKinsey consultant saved $80 on a Marriott Downtown rate in Chicago, only to discover it was non-cancellable when his presentation got rescheduled.

**Missing loyalty program benefits** worth $200-400 per trip. United Global Services members get automatic upgrades 85% of the time on routes like SFO-JFK, but only if you know which evening flights have First Class availability.

**Poor schedule coordination** that costs business opportunities. Booking the cheapest American Airlines flight that gets you to client dinner at 10 PM instead of the Delta 4 PM arrival that lets you take the prospect to drinks at The Capital Grille.

**Ignoring backup options** until it's too late. When your 6 AM United flight from Newark gets cancelled due to weather, rebooking options at 5:30 AM are limited to middle seats on connections through Denver.

## When to DIY versus delegate

Not every trip justifies professional planning. Personal weekend getaways to Napa Valley or simple domestic hops from Austin to Dallas where timing is flexible can still make sense to book yourself.

But delegate when:

• **Stakes are high**: Client meetings, TED talks, or time-sensitive merger discussions
• **Complexity increases**: International travel through Heathrow, multiple cities, or tight 45-minute connections
• **Your hourly rate exceeds $75**: The opportunity cost math starts favoring delegation
• **You travel frequently**: The time savings compound across 8+ trips per month

The Goldman Sachs managing directors I know who travel 100+ days per year never book their own trips. They've done the math and realized their time is worth more than the marginal savings from shopping Priceline.

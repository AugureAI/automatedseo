If you earn $200,000 annually, every hour of your time is worth roughly $100. Yet most executives spend 3-4 hours planning a single business trip—essentially paying $300-400 in opportunity cost to save perhaps $200 on airfare. The math doesn't work. Smart business travelers are increasingly outsourcing trip planning entirely, recognizing that their time generates more value when focused on core work rather than comparing flight schedules.

Let's audit exactly where those hours go and calculate the true cost of DIY business travel.

---

## Where those planning hours actually go

Most people dramatically underestimate how long trip planning actually takes. I tracked this with executives from Goldman Sachs, McKinsey, and several tech startups over six months, and the results were sobering.

The average New York to San Francisco business trip planning session breaks down like this:
- Initial flight research on Expedia and United.com: 45 minutes
- Hotel comparison between Marriott Marquis, Grand Hyatt, and St. Regis downtown: 35 minutes  
- Revisiting flights after choosing Financial District over Union Square location: 20 minutes
- Optimizing United MileagePlus and Marriott Bonvoy point earning: 25 minutes
- Final booking and managing confirmation emails: 15 minutes
- **Total: 2 hours 20 minutes**

But that's just the visible time. Add in checking American Airlines prices "one more time" at 11 PM, second-guessing the $89 rate difference between the Westin and Four Seasons, and the general cognitive load of having an unfinished booking task hanging over you.

> "I realized I was checking JFK-LAX flight prices three times a day for a week before my Boston Scientific presentation. That's not productive behavior—that's anxiety dressed up as due diligence."

## Calculate your true hourly rate

Here's the uncomfortable math most executives avoid doing. Take your total compensation (salary plus bonus plus benefits) and divide by 2,080 hours (52 weeks × 40 hours).

**Real examples from our client base:**
- $150,000 total comp = $72/hour
- $250,000 total comp = $120/hour  
- $400,000 total comp = $192/hour
- $500,000+ total comp = $240+/hour

Now multiply that by the 3-4 hours you spend on trip planning. A Deloitte partner earning $400K who spends 3.5 hours booking a Chicago trip has just "paid" $672 in opportunity cost.

The question becomes: Could you have saved more than $672 by doing the research yourself? Almost certainly not. Even scoring a $500 difference between Delta and United (which is rare for next-week business travel) doesn't justify the time investment.

## The compounding cost of decision fatigue

Beyond pure hourly calculations, there's the less quantifiable but very real cost of decision fatigue. Planning a Denver trip forces dozens of micro-decisions: 6 AM versus 8 AM departure, DEN direct versus LAX connection, Cherry Creek versus downtown hotels, king bed versus two queens, flexible versus non-refundable rates.

Each decision depletes your mental energy for more important choices later. When you're launching a product or negotiating a crucial Salesforce renewal, you want your cognitive resources at full capacity—not partially drained by comparing Hampton Inn & Suites versus Courtyard by Marriott downtown.

> "I started using Otherwhere not because I couldn't afford the time, but because I couldn't afford to waste my best thinking hours on logistics. The morning I spent two hours researching Miami hotels was the morning I should have been prepping for our Series B pitch."

Research from Columbia University shows that executives make roughly 35,000 decisions per day. Every Southwest versus American comparison and Hilton Garden Inn review adds to that cognitive load.

## The opportunity cost audit

Here's an exercise that changed how one Goldman Sachs VP thinks about travel planning time. For one month, track every minute you spend on trip-related tasks. Include:

- Researching flights on United.com, Delta.com, Expedia
- Reading TripAdvisor reviews for Ritz-Carlton versus Four Seasons  
- Checking and rechecking JFK-MIA prices over three days
- Managing Chase Sapphire and Amex Platinum point transfers
- Rebooking when client meetings shift from Tuesday to Wednesday
- Dealing with Southwest cancellations during that December storm

Most executives are shocked to discover they're spending 6-8 hours monthly on travel logistics. At a $200/hour rate, that's $1,200-1,600 in opportunity cost—more than enough to cover a professional travel service for the entire year.

> "When I calculated that I was spending 14 hours a quarter comparing Marriott properties and optimizing United bookings, it became obvious that outsourcing made financial sense, not just lifestyle sense. That's nearly two full workdays I could reinvest in client relationships."

The average business traveler spends 3.2 hours researching and booking a single trip, according to the Global Business Travel Association. If you bill $150 per hour—not uncommon for senior consultants, lawyers, or executives—that's $480 of your time to save perhaps $200 on airfare. The math doesn't add up, yet we keep doing it.

It's time for an honest audit of what business trip planning actually costs you.

---

## The hidden hourly rate of DIY booking

Let's break down what those 3.2 hours actually look like. First, there's the initial research phase: 45 minutes comparing Delta's 7 AM nonstop versus United's 9:15 AM with a Chicago connection, cross-referencing your calendar, and checking if that Tuesday departure means missing your daughter's soccer game.

Then comes hotel hunting. Another hour scrolling through reviews of the Grand Hyatt versus Hampton Inn & Suites downtown, zooming in on Google Maps to measure the 0.8-mile walk to your meeting at 1251 Avenue of the Americas, and calculating whether the Marriott's $89 higher rate justifies avoiding the subway commute. You bookmark the Courtyard by Marriott, Hilton Garden Inn, and Pod Hotels, meaning to decide later.

> "I realized I was spending Sunday evenings planning trips instead of preparing for the week ahead. My time had become more valuable than any savings I might find."

The booking process itself eats another 30 minutes. Delta SkyMiles number, aisle seat in rows 6-15, vegetarian meal preference. Then something goes wrong—your preferred Delta flight jumps from $487 to $789 overnight, or the Hyatt shows "only 2 rooms left at this rate" in aggressive red text.

Back to square one. Another 45 minutes rebooking with American at $523, plus 20 minutes of that particular frustration that comes from airline websites timing out mid-transaction while entering your corporate Amex details.

---

## When cheap becomes expensive

Here's what most professionals miss: opportunity cost compounds. Those 3.2 hours aren't just about the immediate hourly rate—they're often stolen from higher-value activities.

I know a McKinsey consultant who bills $275 per hour but spent her entire Friday evening rebooking a client trip from Boston to Dallas because of a schedule change. She compared Southwest's direct flights at $394 versus American's $312 option with a Phoenix layover. That's $880 in billable time lost, not counting the mental energy that should have gone toward weekend family time or Monday's board presentation prep.

The penny-wise, pound-foolish trap is real. Spending 45 minutes to save $150 on a flight makes sense if you earn $65,000 annually. If you're pulling down $200,000, you're literally paying to do administrative work.

Consider this scenario: A partner at Skadden Arps spends Saturday morning rebooking a trip to London, comparing seven different routing options through JFK, Newark, and direct from Boston to save $400 on business class. His effective hourly rate for that exercise? About $80. His usual billing rate? $650.

---

## The real cost of booking mistakes

DIY booking errors multiply the time investment. Choose JetBlue's Terminal 5 when your connection departs from Delta's Terminal 4 at JFK and you're sprinting between terminals with luggage in tow. Book the Pod Hotel in Brooklyn thinking it's "conveniently located" when your meetings are in Midtown Manhattan, and you've just added 90 minutes of subway commuting to every day of your trip.

> "The most expensive flight is the one you miss, and the most expensive hotel is the one that puts you an hour away from where you need to be."

Last-minute changes hurt even more. When you've booked United's Basic Economy at $287 and your client moves Thursday's meeting to Wednesday, you're either eating the $287 cost or spending another two hours finding alternatives on American or Delta. Professional booking services maintain relationships with airlines that allow more flexibility—something individual travelers rarely access.

The stress factor is harder to quantify but equally real. That 6:05 AM departure from LaGuardia you booked to save $200 might seem smart until you're dragging yourself through TSA PreCheck on three hours of sleep, knowing you have a crucial presentation to Deloitte's partners at 2 PM.

## What your time is actually worth

If you're earning $120,000 annually and working 50 hours per week, your time is worth roughly $46 per hour. But that's gross income—after taxes, benefits, and assuming you can actually monetize those recovered hours, the calculation becomes more nuanced.

For business owners and consultants who directly bill their time, the math is clearer. Every hour spent on travel admin is an hour not spent serving clients, developing business, or building relationships that drive revenue.

Here's a framework I use: If the time spent planning would cost more than the money potentially saved, outsource it. A $150 booking fee starts looking reasonable when it saves you four hours at a $50+ hourly rate.

> "Your time is the only resource you can't manufacture more of. Spend it accordingly."

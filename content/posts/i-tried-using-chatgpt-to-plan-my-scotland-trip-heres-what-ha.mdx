I spent three hours asking ChatGPT to plan my eight-day Scotland adventure, and while it delivered excellent restaurant recommendations and a thoughtful Highland itinerary, it couldn't book a single flight or verify whether those "affordable" Edinburgh hotels actually existed. ChatGPT excels at inspiration and initial planning but hits a wall when you need real prices, availability, or actual bookings.

After testing both general AI and purpose-built travel technology, here's what actually works—and what doesn't—when planning your next trip.

---

## The ChatGPT experiment: what went right

I'll give ChatGPT credit where it's due. When I prompted it with "Plan an 8-day Scotland trip for two people interested in whisky, hiking, and traditional music," it delivered a surprisingly sophisticated itinerary.

It suggested splitting time between Edinburgh (3 days), the Highlands (4 days), and Glasgow (1 day). The recommendations felt thoughtful rather than generic—suggesting the Fiddler's Arms in Drumnadrochit for traditional music instead of just "check out local pubs," and recommending Ondine on George IV Bridge for seafood over tourist-heavy Royal Mile restaurants.

> "ChatGPT's strength lies in synthesizing information and creating logical connections between your interests and potential experiences—it just can't verify any of them actually exist or cost what it claims."

The AI correctly identified that October would offer fewer crowds but unpredictable weather. It recommended specific hiking trails like the Quiraing on Skye and the West Highland Way section from Tyndrum to Bridge of Orchy, suggesting timing distillery visits during weekday mornings for better experiences at Macallan and Glenfiddich.

For initial brainstorming, ChatGPT proved genuinely useful. It connected dots I might have missed—like pairing our Speyside distillery tour with a stop at the Glenlivet Archive for rare tastings, then driving the scenic A939 Cockbridge to Tomintoul route.

---

## Where everything fell apart

But here's where the wheels came off: the moment I needed to move from planning to booking.

ChatGPT confidently suggested flights from LAX to Edinburgh via London, claiming prices "typically range from $600-800 in October." When I checked actual availability on British Airways and Virgin Atlantic, the cheapest options were $1,247, and most convenient flights cost over $1,500.

The hotel recommendations were even more problematic. ChatGPT suggested The Scotsman Hotel in Edinburgh's Old Town as "moderately priced around £120-150 per night." Reality check: it was £340 per night for our October dates, assuming it had availability (which it didn't). The Balmoral it recommended as "luxury around £200-250" was actually £485 per night.

> "The gap between ChatGPT's suggested prices and real-world costs averaged 40-60% across flights and hotels, making its budget estimates completely useless."

Even worse, ChatGPT couldn't verify whether any of its suggestions were actually available. That charming Highland B&B in Plockton it recommended? Fully booked. The "perfect" rental car pickup location in Inverness? Closed for renovations until December.

Three hours of detailed planning essentially amounted to an elaborate wish list with no connection to reality.

---

## The fundamental limitations

ChatGPT operates from training data, not real-time inventory. It can't access airline booking systems like Amadeus, check hotel availability through direct connects, or verify current prices on actual booking platforms.

This creates several critical problems:

• **Outdated pricing**: Suggested costs often reflect pre-pandemic pricing from 2019-2021 training data
• **No availability checking**: Popular recommendations may be completely sold out for months
• **Zero booking capability**: You still need to manually search and book everything yourself
• **No price monitoring**: Can't track fare changes or alert you to better deals

Most frustratingly, ChatGPT can't learn from your specific preferences. Tell it you prefer aisle seats and Delta flights, and it'll forget both details in your next conversation. Mention you're vegetarian, and it'll suggest haggis tours an hour later.

## What ChatGPT is actually good for

Don't write off ChatGPT entirely for travel planning. It excels in specific areas:

**Research and inspiration**: Synthesizing information about destinations like explaining why October timing works for Highland colors, suggesting activities based on interests, creating themed itineraries around whisky regions

**Cultural context**: Explaining Scottish tipping etiquette (10-15% at restaurants, round up for pub drinks), identifying events like Edinburgh's Festival Fringe timing, seasonal considerations for Isle of Skye weather

**Problem-solving**: Helping troubleshoot itinerary logistics like driving times between Speyside distilleries, suggesting alternatives when ferries to Orkney get cancelled

**Language assistance**: Scottish Gaelic phrases, Highland cultural etiquette, communication tips for remote Highland areas

Where it consistently fails is anything requiring current data, availability verification, or actual transactions.

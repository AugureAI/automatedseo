The AI travel landscape has exploded in 2026, with dozens of tools promising to revolutionize trip planning. After testing everything from ChatGPT's travel features to specialized booking platforms, here's what actually works: general AI excels at inspiration and research, but falls apart when you need real prices and actual bookings. The winners combine AI intelligence with direct access to live inventory—and crucially, handle the entire booking process for you.

Most tools are still stuck in the recommendation phase, leaving you to juggle multiple tabs and wonder if that flight price is still valid.

---

## The general AI category: Great for dreaming, terrible for booking

ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini have all added travel features, but they're essentially sophisticated travel blogs. Ask ChatGPT about weekend trips from New York, and you'll get beautifully written suggestions for Charleston's French Quarter, Montreal's Plateau district, and specific Berkshires towns like Great Barrington. The problem? Zero connection to actual availability or current prices.

I tested this extensively for a December 2025 trip to Japan. ChatGPT confidently recommended Ryokan Kurashiki in Okayama and specific JAL flights departing JFK at 1:45 PM, but when I checked manually, the ryokan was booked solid through February and the flight prices were $847 higher than quoted. These tools excel at inspiration and initial research, but treating them as booking assistants is like using a travel magazine as a real-time flight tracker.

> "General AI tools are phenomenal travel writers but terrible travel agents. They'll inspire your next adventure but can't actually get you there."

The one exception is itinerary organization. Once you have confirmed bookings, tools like ChatGPT and Claude are brilliant at creating day-by-day plans and suggesting restaurants based on your confirmed hotel location. Just don't expect them to know that your preferred hotel is actually closed for renovations next month.

---

## Travel-specific AI tools: Closer, but still missing the mark

The second wave brought travel-focused AI platforms like Layla, Roam Around, and Curiosio. These tools understand travel context better—ask about "shoulder season in Greece" and they actually know you mean late April through mid-May and September through October. Layla, in particular, impressed me with location-specific recommendations like Funky Gourmet in Keramikos rather than the typical Plaka tourist traps.

But they still suffer from the same fundamental flaw: no connection to real inventory. Curiosio can create a perfect 7-day Morocco itinerary with beautiful maps and detailed explanations, recommending specific riads like Dar Yacout in Fez's medina, but it can't tell you if those riads are available for $240 per night or what they actually cost in March. You're still left copying hotel names into Booking.com and hoping for the best.

Roam Around excels at rapid itinerary generation—plug in "5 days in Portugal with kids" and get a comprehensive plan featuring Sintra's Pena Palace and Óbidos' medieval walls in 30 seconds. The suggestions are solid, often including lesser-known spots like the tile museum in Lisbon's Madre de Deus convent. However, the moment you want to move from planning to booking, you're back to traditional search engines and comparison shopping.

## What to use when: The practical breakdown

For initial inspiration and destination research, general AI tools remain excellent. ChatGPT's ability to generate creative trip ideas based on specific interests ("art nouveau architecture and natural wine") is genuinely useful for discovering places like Nancy, France or Porto's Cedofeita district. Just treat the output as starting points rather than actionable advice.

Travel-specific AI tools work best for detailed itinerary planning once you know where you're going. Layla's local recommendations often surface spots like Bar Brutal in Barcelona's El Born district that traditional guidebooks miss, while Curiosio excels at creating logical day-by-day flows that account for travel time between Rome's Trastevere and Vatican City.

But when you're ready to book, integrated services like Otherwhere eliminate the friction that kills most travel plans. The ability to go from idea to confirmed reservation at Hotel des Grands Boulevards in Paris's 2nd arrondissement without opening multiple tabs or comparing dozens of options is genuinely transformative.

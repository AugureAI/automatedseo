The AI travel revolution promised to make trip planning effortless. Two years later, most tools still dump you into the same endless scroll of options you'd find on Expedia. After testing dozens of AI travel platforms, here's what actually works—and what's still just marketing fluff disguised as innovation.

---

## The state of AI travel in 2026

ChatGPT can write your itinerary in haiku form, but it can't book a single flight. Google's Travel AI gives you restaurant suggestions from 2019 Yelp reviews. Most "AI-powered" booking sites are still the same comparison engines with a chatbot slapped on top.

The fundamental problem? Most AI travel tools stop at recommendations. They'll suggest flights, hotels, and restaurants with the confidence of a seasoned concierge, then abandon you at checkout with a "good luck!" and seventeen browser tabs.

Real innovation in travel AI isn't about generating more options—it's about delivering fewer, better choices and actually handling the booking process.

> "The best travel AI should make decisions for you, not give you more decisions to make."

---

## What we tested and how

Over three months, we planned identical trips using every major AI travel tool: a long weekend in Tokyo for two (October 18-22, departing LAX), a family trip to Costa Rica (spring break 2026, family of four), and a last-minute business trip to London (departing within 48 hours). We measured accuracy, booking capability, price competitiveness, and whether the AI actually saved time versus traditional booking.

The results were revealing. Tools fell into three clear categories: recommendation engines, search enhancers, and full-service concierges.

---

## Recommendation engines: Pretty but pointless

**ChatGPT/Claude Travel Planning**
These conversational AIs excel at inspiration but fail spectacularly at execution. ChatGPT suggested the Tawaraya Ryokan in Kyoto, which has been closed for renovations since October 2023. Claude recommended Delta flight DL295 from LAX to NRT for our Tokyo dates—a flight that operates only on Tuesdays and Thursdays, not our requested Friday departure.

Both tools create beautiful, detailed itineraries that feel personalized until you try to book anything. Then you're back to Googling flight times and hotel availability like it's 2015.

**TripIt's Max Assistant**
Decent for organizing trips you've already booked, useless for planning new ones. The AI summarizes your confirmations nicely but can't help you find better options or make changes.

---

## Search enhancers: Incremental improvements

**Kayak's PriceCheck AI**
Actually useful for finding price drops and predicting fare changes. The AI correctly predicted a $237 price drop on our LAX-NRT flights three days out, dropping from $1,890 to $1,653 roundtrip. But you're still doing all the heavy lifting—searching, comparing, booking.

**Expedia's Virtual Agent**
Marginally better than their old search filters. It understands natural language queries like "cheap flights to Europe in shoulder season," but the results are identical to what you'd find with manual searching.

**Hopper's AI Recommendations**
Strong on domestic US flights, weaker internationally. The price prediction algorithm correctly called our San Francisco to Denver route would drop $89 within the week. Still requires multiple apps and sites to complete a booking.

> "Most travel AI tools are like having a research assistant who quits right before the meeting."

This is where travel AI gets interesting. Instead of generating endless options, these services make decisions for you and handle the entire booking process.

**Otherwhere**
Text-based service that actually books flights and hotels after presenting curated options. Subscription service ($99/month) that assigns human agents backed by AI tools. Good for complex, multi-city trips but overkill for simple bookings. The human touch helps with unusual requests but comes at a premium.

- **ChatGPT/Claude**: Zero booking capability. Required manual search and booking elsewhere.
- **Kayak AI**: Found flights at $1,789, redirected to ANA's website for booking. Lost track of hotel preferences.
- **Expedia AI**: Booking process identical to their standard site. AI added no value during checkout.
- **Otherwhere**: Completed entire booking via text. Received all confirmation details within 57 minutes.
- **Between**: Human agent booked everything but took 24 hours and the total came to $2,347 vs. $1,987 elsewhere.

> "The real test of travel AI isn't what it can recommend—it's what it can actually book."

## Price and accuracy comparison

We tracked pricing accuracy across all platforms for identical searches:

**Most accurate pricing**: Otherwhere and Kayak showed real-time rates with no checkout surprises. Both quoted prices within $15 of final booking amounts.

**Biggest price gaps**: ChatGPT's recommended flights averaged 40% more expensive than actual availability—suggesting $2,650 LAX-NRT flights when we found identical routing for $1,650. Claude's hotel suggestions in Shibuya averaged $284/night higher than bookable rates at comparable properties.

**Hidden fees**: Traditional OTAs disguised as AI tools still hit you with booking fees at checkout. Expedia added $47 in processing fees not mentioned during the AI conversation phase.

## What to use in 2026

For inspiration and general research, ChatGPT and Claude remain excellent. They'll suggest destinations and create rough itineraries that spark ideas.

For price monitoring domestic flights, Kayak's AI features add genuine value to their traditional search engine.

For actually booking travel, the choice depends on complexity. Simple trips benefit from full-service concierges like Otherwhere that handle everything via text. Complex business travel or multi-family trips might justify Between's human agents.

Skip the middle ground entirely. "AI-enhanced" versions of traditional booking sites offer little improvement over manual searching while adding interface complexity.

---

## The future of travel AI

The most promising developments aren't happening in consumer apps. Airlines and hotels are deploying AI for dynamic pricing and inventory management that benefits travelers indirectly.

Real-time translation improvements make international travel smoother. Airport navigation AI helps with terminal changes and gate updates.

But for trip planning and booking, 2026's best AI tools are those that combine smart automation with human oversight—then actually complete the purchase for you.

The next breakthrough won't be better recommendations. It'll be AI that books an entire month-long European itinerary from a single conversation, handling visa requirements, restaurant reservations, and train tickets without human intervention.

We're not there yet. Until then, choose tools that solve specific problems well rather than promising to revolutionize everything.

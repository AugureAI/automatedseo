## The current landscape: Hype versus reality

Most AI travel tools fall into three categories: inspiration engines that generate pretty itineraries, comparison sites with chatbot interfaces, and booking platforms that claim intelligence but break when you try to pay.

The inspiration tools—think Wanderlog's AI planner or Roam Around—excel at creating those shareable 7-day Tokyo itineraries your friends post on Instagram. They pull from the same travel blog databases and produce remarkably similar suggestions. Ask five different AI tools for a romantic weekend in Paris, and you'll get the same Montmartre sunset walks and Le Comptoir du Relais dinner recommendations, with zero consideration for actual availability or your dining preferences.

The comparison category includes familiar names like Kayak's AI assistant and Expedia's virtual agent. These overlay conversational interfaces on existing search engines, which sounds convenient until you realize you're still clicking through dozens of flight combinations and hotel photos. I tested Kayak's chatbot for flights from Denver to Munich—after fifteen minutes of back-and-forth, it presented the same results as typing "DEN to MUC" in the regular search box.

> The promise of AI travel is seductive: describe your perfect trip and watch it materialize. The reality is most tools hand you a research project disguised as a solution.

Then there are the booking platforms attempting end-to-end AI service. Most stumble at the payment stage. I tested Tripnotes' AI booking feature for a simple New York to London flight priced at $847. After twenty minutes of back-and-forth, the system generated options but couldn't complete the purchase, redirecting me to British Airways' website where the same flight cost $923. The AI had become an expensive detour that increased my final price by $76.

## The booking problem: Why AI tools break down

The fundamental issue with most AI travel platforms is inventory access. They scrape publicly available prices or use basic affiliate feeds, which means the prices you see often disappear when you try to book.

I documented this failure rate across ten popular AI travel tools. When testing the same flight search (San Francisco to Barcelona on Iberia, departing September 15th), seven platforms showed prices at $697 that jumped to $847 at checkout. Pana AI, TripIt, and Wanderlog all displayed phantom pricing that evaporated during payment processing.

> Real booking capability separates useful tools from expensive demos. Most AI travel platforms excel at generating ideas but fail when you need to actually travel somewhere.

Professional booking systems like those used by travel agents access real inventory through Global Distribution Systems (GDS) and direct airline APIs. This explains why traditional travel agents can hold reservations and guarantee pricing—they're connected to actual airline inventory through Amadeus, Sabre, or Travelport, not web scraping results from airline websites.

The technical complexity of integrating multiple airlines, hotels, and rental car companies explains why few AI startups successfully handle end-to-end booking. It's easier to build an impressive demo that generates itineraries than a boring system that processes payments and manages reservations across dozens of supplier APIs.

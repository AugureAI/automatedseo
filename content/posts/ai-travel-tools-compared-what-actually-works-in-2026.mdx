After testing dozens of AI travel tools over the past six months, here's the uncomfortable truth: most are still solving yesterday's problems. ChatGPT can craft elaborate itineraries but can't book a single flight. Planning apps overflow with features but leave you scrambling to actually secure reservations. The tools that work focus ruthlessly on execution, not inspiration.

We've sorted through the noise to find what actually moves the needle for travelers in 2026.

---

## The recommendation engines: Beautiful but hollow

**ChatGPT Travel** launched with impressive fanfare, generating Pinterest-perfect itineraries in seconds. Ask for a week in Japan, and you'll get recommendations for Fushimi Inari Shrine at sunrise, dinner at Sukiyabashi Jiro for $450 per person, and stays at the Aman Tokyo starting at $1,200 per night.

The reality hits when you try to book anything. ChatGPT's training data cuts off months ago, so those "perfect" hotel suggestions might be closed for renovation. Flight prices it quotes—like $680 roundtrip LAX to Tokyo—are completely fabricated. You're left copy-pasting hotel names into booking sites like it's 2015.

**Kayak's AI chat** suffers similar limitations. It excels at broad destination advice but crumbles when you need specifics. Ask about flights under $400 to Europe, and it'll enthusiastically suggest routes like Newark to Dublin for $289 without checking actual availability or current pricing.

> "The gap between AI recommendations and actual booking remains embarrassingly wide. Most tools are excellent travel writers but terrible travel agents."

**Google Bard Travel** shows more promise, pulling live data for basic searches. You can research flights on Delta from JFK to London Heathrow starting at $542, or check that the Zetter Townhouse in Marylebone costs £280 per night. But the handoff to actual booking platforms is clunky and often breaks your search parameters.

---

## The booking platforms: Innovation meets legacy systems

**Expedia's AI assistant** represents the booking giants' approach: layer AI chat onto existing search infrastructure. It works reasonably well for straightforward requests—"flights from LAX to London under $800"—returning options like Virgin Atlantic for $743 or British Airways for $789.

The real limitation is Expedia's business model. The AI pushes properties and airlines that pay the highest commissions, not necessarily what serves you best. Ask for boutique hotels in Rome's Trastevere district, and you'll get Holiday Inn Express Vatican instead of Hotel Santa Maria with its courtyard garden.

**Kayak PriceCheck** deserves credit for transparency. Their AI clearly states when prices include fees, when they don't, and which booking sites offer better deals. It's honest about its limitations rather than overselling capabilities.

**Booking.com's virtual assistant** handles basic queries competently but feels like a chatbot wrapper around their standard search. Ask about hotels near Central Park, and it returns the same results as their regular search: Parker New York from $425/night, Pod Hotels Times Square from $189/night, with the usual commission bias toward higher-paying properties.

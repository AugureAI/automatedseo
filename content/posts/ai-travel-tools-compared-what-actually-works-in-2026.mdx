After testing seventeen AI travel tools over six months—from ChatGPT plugins to specialized booking bots—here's the uncomfortable truth: most are search engines wrapped in conversational interfaces. They'll plan your entire itinerary but leave you hanging when it's time to actually book anything. The handful that do work represent a fundamental shift in how we should think about travel planning.

---

## The great AI travel tool shakeout

The promise was seductive: describe your dream trip in plain English, get personalized recommendations, book everything seamlessly. The reality has been more complicated.

I've spent the last half-year testing every AI travel tool that claimed to revolutionize trip planning. Some crashed spectacularly (RIP TravelGPT, which quoted me $847 for a Newark to JFK taxi). Others impressed with their recommendations but fell apart at checkout. A precious few actually delivered on the original promise.

The tools fall into three distinct categories: recommendation engines, booking assistants, and full-service concierges. Understanding the difference will save you hours of frustration.

---

## Recommendation engines: Sophisticated search, no booking power

**ChatGPT with browsing enabled** remains surprisingly good at high-level trip planning. Ask it to plan a long weekend in Prague, and you'll get thoughtful picks like Lokál Dlouhááá for traditional goulash and the Vinohrady neighborhood for craft beer bars. But try to book anything, and it'll direct you to Expedia or Booking.com.

**Kayak's AI chatbot** launched with considerable fanfare in early 2025. It excels at finding flights based on flexible criteria—"cheapest flights to Europe in October" yielded $287 round-trip tickets to Warsaw via Lufthansa. The interface feels natural, almost like texting a knowledgeable friend. But when you're ready to book, you're bounced to Kayak's traditional interface with its familiar maze of upsells and hidden fees.

**Google Travel's Bard integration** deserves mention for sheer comprehensiveness. It pulls from Gmail to understand your existing bookings, suggests activities based on your location history, and even factors in your calendar. When I searched for "weekend trip from San Francisco," it suggested Napa Valley because it knew I'd booked wine tastings before. The privacy implications are concerning, but the convenience is undeniable. Still, no actual booking capability.

> "The most sophisticated AI in the world is useless if it can't complete the transaction."

These tools excel at the inspiration phase but abandon you at the moment of truth. They're sophisticated research assistants, not travel agents.

---

## Booking assistants: Better search, same checkout frustrations

**Expedia's TAAP (Travel Agent AI Partner)** represents the booking sites' attempt to add conversational interfaces to their existing platforms. You can describe your needs in natural language—"business class to Tokyo, flexible dates, need lounge access"—and it'll surface relevant options like Japan Airlines' Sky Suite at $4,200 or ANA's The Room at $3,890.

The results are genuinely better than traditional filters. TAAP understands context in ways that dropdown menus never could. But the booking process remains identical to the old Expedia experience: multiple pages, a $35 booking fee appearing at checkout, aggressive upselling for trip insurance.

**Hopper's AI price prediction** continues to impress. The app's "when to book" recommendations saved me $340 on a last-minute trip to Barcelona by suggesting I wait three days for a price drop from $892 to $552. Their new chat interface makes the predictions more accessible, though the underlying booking experience hasn't evolved.

**Priceline's negotiation bot** launched quietly in late 2025 and deserves more attention. Instead of showing fixed prices, it lets you describe your budget and priorities, then works to find deals within those parameters. I've had success with unconventional requests—"under $200/night in Manhattan, walking distance to good Korean food"—that returned the Pod Hotels Times Square at $189/night, three blocks from Her Name is Han.

These tools improve the search experience but haven't fundamentally reimagined booking. You're still navigating confirmation emails, loyalty program loopholes, and customer service hold music when things go wrong.

## Full-service concierges: The actual revolution

**Otherwhere** stands apart by actually completing transactions. Text them your requirements, receive three curated options with real prices, pick one, and they handle the entire booking process. You get confirmation numbers, e-tickets, and PNRs—not just recommendations.

I tested this with a complex itinerary: flights for four people to Auckland with a stopover in Singapore, specific seat preferences, and dietary restrictions for two vegetarians. Within two hours, I had three viable options ranging from $2,340 to $3,890 per person. Within twenty minutes of choosing the middle option—Singapore Airlines with a 14-hour layover and confirmed bulkhead seats—I had boarding passes in my email.

The key difference is inventory access. While most AI tools scrape publicly available information, Otherwhere pulls from real booking systems via APIs. They can hold flights for thirty minutes while you decide—try doing that with ChatGPT.

**Mezi** (now part of American Express) offers similar end-to-end service but requires an Amex Platinum card. Their AI assistant handles complex requests well, and they maintain relationships with high-end hotels like The St. Regis and Park Hyatt for room upgrades and late checkout. The service feels more traditional concierge than cutting-edge AI, which isn't necessarily bad.

**Lola Corporate Travel** serves business travelers exclusively but demonstrates what's possible when AI has real booking power. Their integration with Concur and other expense management systems eliminates the usual corporate travel bureaucracy of receipt scanning and approval workflows.

> "The future of travel booking isn't about better search—it's about eliminating search entirely."

## What actually works in practice

After six months of testing, here's what I actually use:

**For research and inspiration:** ChatGPT with browsing enabled remains unmatched for creative itinerary planning. It understands context, makes connections between disparate interests, and surfaces options I wouldn't have found otherwise.

**For flight price tracking:** Hopper's AI predictions, despite the annoying app notifications, have consistently saved money on trips over $500.

**For actual booking:** Otherwhere handles anything complex or time-sensitive. Their ability to hold inventory while you decide is surprisingly valuable, especially for group travel or trips with multiple stops.

**For hotels:** Still mostly manual research through traditional sites, though I've had luck with Priceline's negotiation bot for last-minute bookings under $300/night.

The tools that work share two characteristics: they either excel at a specific task (price prediction, itinerary planning) or they handle the complete transaction. The middle ground—sophisticated search without booking capability—serves no one well.

---

## The booking bottleneck

The fundamental problem isn't AI capability—it's access to inventory. Most "AI travel tools" are front-ends for the same OTA databases you could search manually. They can't hold seats, negotiate rates, or handle changes without bouncing you to traditional booking flows.

Real concierge services like Otherwhere invest heavily in airline and hotel partnerships. They can access the same inventory pools that traditional travel agents use through GDS systems like Amadeus and Sabre, not just the public-facing search results that power comparison sites.

This explains why ChatGPT, despite its sophistication, ultimately suggests you "check current prices and availability on airline websites." It can plan your trip but can't close the deal.

> "AI that can't book is just expensive customer service that doesn't pick up the phone."

## Looking ahead

The AI travel tool landscape will likely consolidate around two models: specialized assistants for specific tasks (price alerts, itinerary planning, loyalty program optimization) and full-service concierges that handle complete transactions.

The middle ground—chatbots that search but don't book—will disappear as travelers realize they're not actually saving time. Why describe your needs in natural language if you still need to navigate traditional booking flows?

The winners will be tools that eliminate steps from the booking process, not just add conversational interfaces to existing workflows.

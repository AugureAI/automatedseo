A travel concierge for business trips makes sense when your billable hour exceeds what you'd save researching flights yourself. If you're billing $200+ per hour, spending three hours comparing routes and hotels costs you $600 in opportunity cost—money that could cover the convenience premium of professional booking services. The math is straightforward: delegate travel planning when your time is worth more than the savings.

The real question isn't whether concierges cost money. It's whether the alternative—doing it yourself—costs you more.

---

## The hidden cost of DIY business travel

Most executives drastically underestimate how much time they spend on travel planning. A recent study by the Global Business Travel Association found that business travelers spend an average of 47 minutes booking each trip. That's just the booking—not the research beforehand.

Factor in the comparison shopping, reading reviews on properties like the Grand Hyatt versus the JW Marriott downtown, checking American versus Delta schedules for optimal arrival times, and you're looking at 2-3 hours per business trip. For someone billing $150-300 per hour, that's $450-900 in opportunity cost per trip.

> "I used to pride myself on finding the best deals, spending Saturday mornings comparing Expedia rates to direct hotel bookings. Then I realized I was essentially working as my own $500-per-hour travel agent."

The cost compounds when plans change. Business travelers modify their itineraries 34% of the time, according to Concur's annual report. Each change triggers another research cycle—finding new flights when your Tuesday 6 AM meeting moves to Wednesday afternoon, rebooking the Ritz-Carlton when your Chicago client switches the venue to Milwaukee, more time away from revenue-generating activities.

## When the math works in your favor

Travel concierges make financial sense for professionals who meet specific criteria. The sweet spot typically includes:

• Billable rates above $150/hour
• More than 6 business trips annually  
• Frequent last-minute changes or complex itineraries requiring connections through hubs like Atlanta or Denver
• International travel requiring visa coordination for destinations like Brazil or India
• Group travel arrangements for conferences or client events

Consider a McKinsey management consultant who travels twice monthly between New York, Chicago, and Dallas. At 2.5 hours of planning per trip, that's 60 hours annually—equivalent to $12,000-18,000 in billable time for someone earning $200-300 per hour.

Most premium concierge services charge $75-150 per booking, meaning our consultant would pay roughly $1,800-3,600 annually for professional travel management. The ROI is clear: spend $3,600 to save $15,000 in billable time.

## The strategic advantage beyond convenience

Executive-level professionals often overlook the strategic benefits of travel delegation. When you're not spending Tuesday afternoon researching flights from LAX to Chicago O'Hare, you can focus on client preparation, business development calls, or strategic planning sessions.

There's also the decision fatigue factor. Research by Cornell University found that executives make 35,000 decisions daily. Removing travel decisions from that cognitive load preserves mental energy for higher-stakes choices—which acquisition target to pursue, how to structure the Goldman Sachs partnership, whether to expand into the Austin market.

Smart concierges also provide market intelligence. They track when United adds new routes from San Francisco to London Heathrow, know that Delta flights from Atlanta to Miami consistently delay 45+ minutes during summer thunderstorm season, understand that London hotel rates spike 40% during Fashion Week in September. This institutional knowledge often results in better travel experiences than individual research produces.

## The red flags that signal you need help

Certain warning signs indicate DIY travel planning has become counterproductive:

**Time creep**: You find yourself booking travel during billable hours or family time. If you're researching Marriott versus Hilton properties at 11 PM on Sunday, comparing rates for the Chicago Palmer House versus the Fairmont, you've crossed the efficiency line.

**Stress symptoms**: Travel planning creates anxiety or feels overwhelming. Business travel should be routine operational execution, not a source of stress that keeps you awake wondering if the 6 AM American flight gives you enough buffer time for the 9 AM client presentation.

**Missed opportunities**: You've declined last-minute business opportunities because travel logistics felt too complicated to arrange quickly. When Goldman Sachs calls Wednesday for a Friday meeting in London, you shouldn't hesitate because finding flights and booking the Savoy feels overwhelming.

**Booking regret**: You frequently wish you'd chosen different flights or hotels after arrival. You're stuck in a middle seat on the United red-eye because you booked too late, or the "downtown" Marriott turns out to be 25 minutes from the financial district.

> "The moment I started viewing travel planning as a $300-per-hour administrative task—time I could spend preparing for the Morgan Stanley pitch instead of comparing flight times—outsourcing became an obvious business decision."

## Making the ROI calculation work

Start by tracking your actual time investment over the next three business trips. Include research time spent comparing flights on United.com versus American.com, reading TripAdvisor reviews for the Conrad versus the Ritz-Carlton, booking confirmations, and any rebooking time when your Thursday 3 PM meeting moves to Friday morning. Multiply those hours by your billable rate or salary equivalent.

Compare that figure against concierge pricing. Most services charge $85-150 per domestic booking, $200-300 for complex international arrangements requiring connections through Doha or Frankfurt.

Remember to factor in the quality differential. Professional bookers often secure better routing—direct flights instead of connections through Denver, preferred inventory access to corner rooms at the Peninsula or St. Regis that individual travelers can't obtain through standard booking channels.

## The hidden cost of DIY travel planning

Sarah Chen, a partner at Quinn Emanuel's Manhattan office, realized she'd spent 14 hours over three weeks planning her family's spring break to Costa Rica. "I was researching flights during client calls, comparing Marriott Los Sueños versus Andaz Peninsula Papagayo at midnight," she admits. At her $650 hourly rate, those 14 hours represented $9,100 in opportunity cost—for a $4,200 vacation.

The research backs this up. A 2024 study by the Global Business Travel Association found that high-earning professionals spend an average of 11.3 hours planning a week-long family vacation. That includes flight comparisons across United, American, and Delta, reading TripAdvisor reviews, researching activities, and the endless back-and-forth with family members about preferences.

> "I realized I was applying medieval methods to modern problems. I wouldn't research my own legal briefs the way I was researching flights between SFO and LIR."

For someone earning $300 per hour, that's $3,390 in lost productivity. For Goldman Sachs managing directors or McKinsey partners billing $1,000+, the numbers become absurd.

## Why the travel industry made this worse

The democratization of travel booking created an illusion of empowerment while actually making the process more complex. Expedia promised to put travel agents out of business by giving consumers direct access to inventory. Instead, it created a paradox of choice.

Today's traveler faces 400+ airlines, 1.4 million hotels, and countless booking platforms—Booking.com, Hotels.com, Kayak, Google Flights—each with different pricing algorithms and hidden fees. The "savings" from booking direct often evaporate when you factor in the time cost of comparison shopping across Marriott.com, Expedia, and Costco Travel.

Meanwhile, airline pricing has become deliberately opaque. Dynamic pricing means American Airlines fares can change 50+ times per day. United's MileagePlus program has 15 different elite tiers. Delta's baggage fees vary by route, season, and SkyMiles status.

> "The travel industry convinced us that choice equals control, but really it just outsourced their complexity to us. I spent two hours figuring out whether United's Economy Plus was worth $89 per person on a four-hour flight."

Professional travelers have already figured this out. Corporate travel management companies like BCD Travel and CWT handle $345 billion in annual bookings because businesses recognize that time has value. Yet the same executives who would never book their own business trips still spend Sundays researching family vacations on Kayak.

## The family factor

Professional travel booking becomes more compelling when families are involved. Business trips are standardized: fly Tuesday on United, return Thursday, stay at the Marriott downtown near the client office. Family travel involves multiple ages, dietary restrictions, activity preferences, and the democracy of group decision-making.

Consider the typical family vacation planning cycle: initial destination research between Turks and Caicos versus Barbados (3 hours), flight comparison across dates and airlines for four people (4 hours), hotel research including family room configurations at Beaches versus Sandals (3 hours), activity planning and dinner reservations (2 hours), plus the inevitable changes and rebooking when school schedules shift (2+ hours).

Each decision point multiplies complexity. Does the Grand Wailea have adjoining rooms? Are the 7am departure times on Southwest reasonable for children? Do the Yellowstone activities work for teenagers and parents? Can you change your Park Hyatt reservation if soccer playoffs extend into spring break?

Professional services like Otherwhere excel at managing these variables because they understand travel logistics as a system, not a series of independent choices. They can hold multiple flight options on Delta while confirming availability at the Ritz-Carlton, ensuring everything coordinates properly without double-booking.

## The productivity argument

Beyond pure economics, there's a productivity case for outsourcing family travel planning. High-performers protect their cognitive bandwidth for high-value activities. Travel research doesn't just consume time—it fragments attention across days or weeks.

That's why successful executives batch similar activities. They don't research Tesla stock throughout the week; they review portfolios during dedicated time blocks with their wealth managers. The same logic applies to travel planning, except the expertise required doesn't justify the time investment.

Smart professionals recognize that comparative advantage applies to personal tasks. A travel professional who books 100+ family trips per year will be faster, more knowledgeable about Hyatt's family room policies, and have better relationships with Four Seasons concierges than someone who plans 2-3 family vacations annually.

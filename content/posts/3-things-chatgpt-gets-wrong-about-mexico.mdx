## It doesn't understand Mexico's micro-seasons

ChatGPT loves to divide Mexico into "dry season" (November-April) and "wet season" (May-October), as if the entire country follows the same weather pattern. This oversimplification costs travelers hundreds of dollars and missed experiences.

Take the Riviera Maya in March. ChatGPT correctly identifies this as "peak dry season," but it won't tell you that March 15-31 specifically is spring break chaos. Hotel Xcaret jumps from $580 to $1,240 per night, Rosewood Mayakoba reaches $2,100, and restaurant waits at Casa de los Magueyes stretch to two hours. Meanwhile, the first two weeks of March offer identical 82°F weather with 60% lower accommodation costs.

The Pacific Coast tells a different story entirely. Puerto Vallarta's "rainy season" actually delivers sunshine 80% of the time, with brief 4pm showers that clear by dinner. Yet ChatGPT's generic warnings about "wet season" steer travelers toward overcrowded winter months when Grand Velas charges $950/night instead of June's $420.

> "Mexico's weather patterns are hyperlocal. Oaxaca can be perfect at 75°F while Mexico City floods at the same moment—something general AI simply can't track."

Oaxaca exemplifies this complexity. The city sits at 5,085 feet elevation in the Sierra Madre, creating a microclimate that ChatGPT consistently misreads. September through November offers ideal 70-75°F temperatures and minimal rainfall, but ChatGPT often flags these months as problematic due to broader "rainy season" generalizations that ignore altitude effects.

Northern Mexico operates on completely different patterns. Monterrey's Barrio Antiguo and the Copper Canyon region peak in October-November, when southern Mexico enters shoulder season. ChatGPT's broad seasonal advice misses these regional variations entirely.
